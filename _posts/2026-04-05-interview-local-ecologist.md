For fifteen years, wetland ecologist Dr. [Name] has waded through the region's floodplains, cataloguing what remains and fighting for what can still be saved. We met her at one of her regular monitoring sites on the lower river to talk about the state of the wetlands and what it would take to turn things around.

**How did you come to focus on freshwater ecosystems?**

I grew up near a river. I spent a lot of time in and around it as a kid. By the time I got to university I already knew what I wanted to study. And then the more I learned, the more urgent it felt. Freshwater biodiversity is collapsing globally — at roughly twice the rate of terrestrial or marine species — and it barely registers in public debate.

**What does a typical monitoring visit look like?**

We check water quality parameters — temperature, turbidity, dissolved oxygen — and we do vegetation surveys and fauna spotting. I've been visiting many of these same sites since 2011. You develop a feel for a place over time. You notice when something's missing.

**What's missing?**

Frogs, mostly. Some of these sites used to be deafening on a spring night. Now you might hear one or two species where there used to be seven or eight. The silence is striking once you know what it used to sound like.

**Is there anything that gives you hope?**

Yes, actually. I've seen sites come back when conditions improve. One floodplain we'd written off after a decade of very poor water allocation — we got two years of good flooding and within 18 months there was vegetation reestablishing, the birds came back, the frogs came back. Wetlands can recover. They just need the chance.
