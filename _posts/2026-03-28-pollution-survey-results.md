A two-year water quality survey has produced the first comprehensive pollution map of the region's river network, revealing elevated nutrient and sediment loads across more than half of all tested sites — with some reaches recording levels more than four times the guideline thresholds.

The survey, conducted by the regional water authority with support from local councils, tested 112 sites across the catchment for a range of indicators including nitrogen, phosphorus, E. coli, turbidity, and heavy metals.

Results showed the most severely affected reaches are in the mid-catchment agricultural zone, where intensive cropping and livestock grazing continue within close proximity to waterways.

## Hotspots identified

Seventeen sites were classified as "high risk," a designation requiring remediation action under state environmental guidelines. Of these, eleven are adjacent to agricultural properties, four are near urban drainage outfalls, and two are downstream of legacy industrial sites.

The survey found that E. coli counts exceeded safe recreational contact guidelines at nine sites during the sampling period — information relevant to local communities that use the river for swimming and recreation.

## What happens next

The water authority says it will use the data to prioritise investment in riparian fencing programs and to engage directly with landholders in the highest-impact areas. Funding applications are being prepared for a targeted on-farm improvement scheme expected to be announced later this year.

Environmental advocates have welcomed the data but say publication alone is insufficient without enforceable outcomes.
