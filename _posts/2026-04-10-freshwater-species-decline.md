A newly published survey of the region's waterways has found that populations of native freshwater species have fallen by an average of 38 per cent over the past 20 years, with some species recorded at less than a fifth of their historical abundance.

The report, compiled by researchers at the local university in partnership with state environment agencies, draws on two decades of biodiversity monitoring data from 43 sites across the catchment.

"These are not abstract statistics," said the lead researcher. "We are talking about species that have been part of this landscape for millions of years, and we are watching them disappear within a single human lifetime."

## Key findings

Among the species hardest hit are the southern pygmy perch, the Murray crayfish, and several freshwater mussel species. Introduced trout and carp continue to outcompete native fish in many reaches, particularly in warmer summer months when low water flow and elevated temperatures favour introduced species.

The report identifies three primary drivers of decline: habitat degradation, altered flow regimes due to upstream water extraction, and the ongoing spread of introduced species.

## What researchers are calling for

The authors recommend an urgent review of water licences in the upper catchment, expanded feral animal control, and investment in fish passage infrastructure at key barriers including two road crossings identified as blocking migration routes.
