A regional conservation group has received a $2.4 million grant to restore native vegetation along the catchment's most degraded riverbanks, marking one of the largest single investments in local freshwater ecosystems in more than a decade.

The funding, announced Tuesday by the state government's environmental department, will support the removal of invasive willows and blackberry and the planting of more than 60,000 native seedlings across 14 kilometres of riverbank over the next three years.

"This river has been in decline for too long," said the group's director. "These funds will let us finally do the restoration work at the scale it requires."

## What the funding covers

The grant will be split across three work streams: vegetation removal, revegetation with indigenous species, and long-term monitoring to track water quality and biodiversity outcomes.

Community volunteers will be involved in planting days scheduled to begin in autumn. Locals interested in participating can register through the group's website.

## Background

The catchment has seen significant degradation over recent decades due to land clearing, agricultural runoff, and the spread of introduced plant species. Native fish populations have declined sharply, with platypus sightings in the lower reaches down more than 60 per cent since monitoring began in the 1990s.

The new funding follows a $480,000 feasibility study completed last year that identified the most critical intervention sites along the river system.
